--- ## Thesis Overview - Autonomic nervous system responds to both acute and chronic stress that can lead to (mal)adaptive changes - Stress can by psychological (e.g. depression) and physiological (e.g. myocardial ischemia) - Both stress and autonomic response can be quantified: - Psychological surveys and symptom severity - Measurement of coronary artery disease - Autonomic function assessed non-invasively through electrocardiogram (ECG) - Relationship between psychological and cardiovascular disease can be studied from perspective of autonomic reactivity --- ## Specific Aims This thesis aims to study the effect of both acute and chronic psychological and physiological stress on autonomic reactivity. The autonomic response to different perturbances may have an overall impact on cardiovascular disease and mortality. -- 1. **To assess the effect of myocardial ischemia and coronary reperfusion on changes in cardiac autonomic activitiy.** -- 1. **To determine if cardiac autonomic activity modifies the relationship between acute and chronic psychological stress and myocardial ischemia.** -- 1. **To explore the association of cardiac autonomic activity with future major adverse cardiovascular events.** --- ## Approach .pull-left[ Critical elements: - Myocardial ischemia using perfusion imaging and coronary angiography - Psychological profiling for depression, anxiety, and mental stress disorders - Measure of cardiac autonomic activity ] .pull-right[ By pulling together several similar studies, we can assess cardiovascular disease status, psychological stress (both acute and chronic), autonomic activity through electrocardiography: - Emory Cardiovascular Biobank - Emory Twins Study - Myocardial Infarction and Mental Stress (MIMS) and Mental Stress Ischemia Mechanisms and Prognosis Study (MIPS) ] --- class: middle # Background --- ## Clinical Relevance - Coronary artery disease (CAD) is the leading cause of death and psychological diseases, e.g. depression, are the leading cause of disability **worldwide** - Cardiovascular disease is comorbid with psychological disease, e.g. 20% of patients with CAD have depression, with subsequent 3 times greater risk of mortality (Meijer, Conradi, Bos, Thombs, van Melle, and de Jonge, 2011; Jha, Qamar, Vaduganathan, Charney, and Murrough, 2019) - Acute mental stress leads to increased risk of myocardial ischemia and is suggestive of worse outcomes(Vaccarino, Sullivan, Hammadah, Wilmot, Al Mheid, Ramadan, Elon, Pimple, Garcia, Nye, Shah, Alkhoder, Levantsevych, Gay, Obideen, Huang, Lewis, Bremner, Quyyumi, and Raggi, 2018) - Chronic stress can lead to autonomic changes over time (Shah, Alonso, Whitsel, Soliman, Vaccarino, and Shah, 2020) - No current interventions, such as antidepressant medications, improve future CAD-related outcomes
.right[This suggests a relationship between the heart and brain exists that may affect cardiovascular outcomes.] --- background-image: url("./refs/2016-shivkumar-neurocardiac-schematic.png") background-size: contain background-position: right ## Neurocardiac Axis .pull-left[ Multiple levels of neural regulation of cardiac physiology: - Intrinsic cardiac nervous system - Intrathoracic cardiac nervous system - Central nervous system Key is that there are both **afferent** and **efferent** systems interaction .footnote[Shivkumar, Ajijola, Anand, Armour, Chen, Esler, De Ferrari, Fishbein, Goldberger, Harper, Joyner, Khalsa, Kumar, Lane, Mahajan, Po, Schwartz, Somers, Valderrabano, Vaseghi, and Zipes (2016)] ] --- background-image: url("./refs/2011-taggart-neurocardiac.png") background-size: contain background-position: right .left-column[


Taggart, Critchley, and Lambiase (2011) outline a hypothetical model of how psychological factors effect the sinoatrial node, the myocardium, and coronary vascular system, described as the **neurocardiac axis**. ] --- ## Heart Rate Variability _Heart rate variability (HRV) can represent the current state of the ANS as a potential "biomarker" of neurocardiac health._ __Heart Rate Fluctuations__ - Fluctuations in the HR are mediated by sympathetic (SNS) and parasympathetic (PNS) inputs to the sinoatrial node - Rapid fluctuations in HR reflect vagal/PNS control - Slower fluctuations in HR reflect SNS and PNS integartion, along with other influences - External influences such as activity, mental stress, etc - Internal period rhythms also exist: RSA, baroreceptor reflexes, circadian rhythm, RAAS/neuroendocrine secretions, etc --- class: middle # Methods --- ### Study Design: Emory Cardiovascular Biobank Cross-sectional design. Patients undergoing cardiac catheterization at EUH are enrolled, excluding those with acute coronary syndromes. Psychological and clinical profiling is done prior to catheterization. ECG data is collected before, during, and after catheterization.* - ECG: Using single-lead patch data (VivaLNK), RR intervals collected throughout procedure to generate HRV. - CAD: Left heart catheterization with coronary angiography performed. If coronary disease was significant, PCI (revascularization) was performed. - $\Psi$: Depressive symptom burden collected via PHQ-9 (Patient Health Questionnaire). .footnote[ *Due to COVID-19, the datasets are reduced in size, and adequate, predetermined power has not yet been achieved. ] --- ### Study Design: Emory Twins Study Observational/cross-sectional design. Veteran twins were enrolled for quantitative MPI. Patients were flown to study site at EUH and monitored for ~30 hours, with controlled schedules. Clinical characteristics and extensive psychological profiling performed. Follow-up data for outcomes collected. - ECG: 3-lead to 12-lead Holter monitors were placed. Data transformed to HRV from raw GE Seer format - CAD: Quantitative MPI performed on all patients, with coronary flow reserve measured - $\Psi$: Depression data collected using BDI, and anxiety/post-traumatic stress disorder collected by SCID - Follow-up: Adjudicated outcome events (MACE, hospitalization, etc) were collected --- ### Study Design: MIMS/MIPS Observational/cross-sectional design. Patients after recent catheterization for chest pain or abnormal MPI were enrolled, primarily Grady and EUH. They returned for first visit within 6 months of event. Mental stress and physical stress performed, followed by quantitative MPI. ECG collected before, during, and after mental stress challenge. Follow-up data for clinical outcomes collected. - ECG: 1-lead to 12-lead ECG monitors were placed. Data transformed to HRV using MATLAB - CAD: Stress test performed initially using either mental/physical stress. Qualitative MPI performed on all patients, with coronary flow reserve measured - $\Psi$: Depression and anxiety data collected using SCID - Follow-up: Adjudicated outcome events (MACE, hospitalization, etc) were collected --- ## Aim 1 #### To assess the effect of myocardial ischemia and coronary reperfusion on changes in cardiac autonomic activity. .pull-left[ *Hypothesis:* 1. Increasing burden of CAD associates with decreased HRV before catheterization and/or intervention. 1. In those with obstructive CAD (stenosis $>$ 70%), HRV is higher after reperfusion of coronary arteries compared to those without intervention. ] .pull-right[ ] --- ### Analytical Plan This aim can be answered by using the data from the **Emory Cardiovascular Biobank**, who receive continuous ECG monitoring and are underoing cardiac catherization. This natural experiment allows for controlling the effect of sedation and intervention within and between patients. Statistics: - *Paired T-test* to compare HRV pre/post PCI - *Linear regression* to measure effect of revascularization on HRV - *Logistic regression* to measure association of prior HRV to predict if PCI is performed - Adjusted models for cardiovascular and psychological risk factors --- ## Aim 2 --- ## Aim 3 --- class: middle # Results --- class: middle, inverse ## Cohort Overview ---
